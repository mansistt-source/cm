STYLE: MONUMENTAL RETRO-FUTURIST 3D

VISUAL IDENTITY:
Create a high-end 3D cinematic world that blends retro-futurist sci-fi, environmental storytelling, monumental scale, and tactile decay. The mood should feel mysterious, exploratory, and slightly melancholic, with a sense of discovery inside abandoned or repurposed technological ruins. Use oversized objects, derelict machines, monolithic geometric structures, stranded pods, industrial relics, consumer-object-inspired forms, overgrown robotics, alien cubes, flooded ruins, desert wrecks, swampy fields, and moonlike or liminal environments. The color palette should alternate between cool cyan-blue atmospheres and warm amber-orange emission, with muted earth browns, oxidized reds, moss greens, pale sky blues, and desaturated metallic grays. Contrast should be strong but not harsh, with light used to carve form and reveal scale. Texture must be physically based and highly tactile: worn paint, rust, moss, dirt, scratches, wet surfaces, corroded metal, weathered plastic, mud, algae, cracked panels, and realistic reflections. The rendering should feel premium 3D, not cartoon-flat or plastic-toy-like.

CINEMATOGRAPHY:
Use cinematic 3D compositions with strong scale contrast between small human figures and massive objects or structures. Favor wide and extreme-wide shots, low-angle hero perspectives, centered compositions, long vanishing approaches, and silhouettes facing large forms. Use occasional medium shots only when highlighting a specific object’s design language or environmental detail. Preferred lens behavior is wide-angle for environment and spectacle, roughly 18mm to 28mm equivalent, with occasional 35mm framing for more intimate or object-focused shots. Camera movement should be slow, deliberate, and exploratory: gradual push-ins, drifting lateral moves, gentle crane motion, orbit reveals, and controlled dolly approaches. Depth of field should be restrained and cinematic; preserve scene legibility and scale rather than blurring the environment excessively. Composition should often use a clear focal monument, a small witness figure, and foreground elements such as puddles, dock planks, grass, debris, cables, reeds, rocks, or reflections to build depth.

LIGHTING:
Lighting must feel atmospheric and physically motivated. Use golden-hour sun, soft overcast daylight, mist-filtered blue light, moonlike ambient fill, luminous interior cores, portal-like glows, soft practical LEDs, and concealed machinery emission. Shadow behavior should be soft to medium-soft in open environments, deeper and more dramatic around interiors, monoliths, or enclosed structures. Atmospheric effects are essential: fog, light haze, dust, suspended moisture, volumetric shafts, faint smoke, reflected glow, and subtle bloom. Water reflections, wet-ground reflections, and metallic bounce light should contribute to the look. Warm emissive cores or windows should contrast with cooler surrounding atmosphere. Avoid flat even lighting, hard studio lighting, or overly glossy commercial highlights.

SCENE STRUCTURE:
Open scenes with a clear establishing shot that immediately reveals an extraordinary object, structure, or environment: an overgrown robot head in a forest pond, a stranded cylindrical machine in a marsh, giant pod-like habitats in shallow water, a derelict spherical shelter in a desert, astronauts approaching an anomaly, a suspended ring or split machine above reflective ground, or a giant alien cube emerging from mist. Pacing should feel slow, observant, and discovery-driven. Reveal information through scale, approach, and atmosphere rather than fast cutting or action choreography. Use environmental transitions such as drifting fog, changing light, water ripple, camera glide, or a slow reveal from foreground obstruction. Close scenes on a lingering frame that preserves mystery: the object remains unexplained, the structure glows from within, the human remains small, and the environment suggests a larger unseen world.

SUBJECT TREATMENT:
Subjects should be framed as explorers, witnesses, or small occupants within a larger designed world. When humans appear, keep them relatively small in frame to emphasize the scale of the environment or object. When the subject is an object or structure, treat it like a story-bearing artifact: massive, worn, partially broken, reclaimed by nature, or quietly active. Consumer-object-inspired forms should be transformed into original sci-fi architecture or machinery rather than shown as clean branded products. Shapes can be whimsical or iconic, but surfaces must be aged, grounded, and believable. Backgrounds should never feel empty; they must reinforce narrative context with terrain, weather, water, vegetation, docking elements, debris, sky, or distant structures. Every object should feel like it has history, use, and environmental interaction.

WHAT TO AVOID:
Avoid flat cartoon rendering, low-detail modeling, overly clean showroom surfaces, generic game-asset appearance, bright toy-like colors, noisy clutter without focal hierarchy, excessive neon cyberpunk styling, weightless floating objects without environmental grounding, shallow depth of field that hides scene design, random sci-fi greebles without purpose, and over-saturated fantasy lighting. Avoid direct imitation of any specific franchise, character, mascot, game world, logo, or copyrighted object design. Do not present exact branded products or official entertainment designs as the hero asset. Avoid empty backgrounds, flat white skies, and action-heavy compositions that break the contemplative exploratory tone. Preserve the style as original cinematic 3D worldbuilding defined by monumental forms, retro-futurist decay, atmospheric lighting, tactile materials, and mystery-driven environmental storytelling.
