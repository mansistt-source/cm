STYLE: FUTURISTIC CINEMATIC ADVERTISING

VISUAL IDENTITY:
Create a premium futuristic advertising style that merges high-end product marketing, cinematic sci-fi worldbuilding, and graphic editorial control. The mood must feel aspirational, designed, technologically advanced, and visually intentional. The style should support three linked modes: polished brand-launch atmosphere, experimental sci-fi lifestyle imagery, and high-intensity action-promo spectacle. Use clean dark backgrounds, controlled environmental sets, glossy or matte tech surfaces, field-grown surreal installations, futuristic uniforms, robotic or cybernetic figures, and immersive action moments. The color palette should be selective and system-based rather than random: deep black, charcoal, and graphite as the base; cool cyan, teal, and aqua for futuristic clarity; vivid neon green for digital-energy moments; magenta and hot pink for hyper-action or neon combat accents; soft white glow for interface and call-to-action moments; and occasional muted yellow or orange for contrast in product/fashion scenes. Saturation may be high in accent lighting but the overall frame should remain curated and premium. Texture should be crisp, high-resolution, and commercially polished, with controlled bloom, reflective highlights, subtle lens artifacts, glass shards, particles, holographic overlays, and clean digital sharpness. Avoid muddy textures, uncontrolled noise, or flat social-media aesthetics.

CINEMATOGRAPHY:
Use ad-grade cinematography with strong intent per shot. Favor a mix of clean hero compositions, editorial triptych or stacked-panel layouts, first-person or near-lens action framing, low-angle dominance shots, and centered symmetrical tech shots. Camera movement should feel designed rather than documentary: slow push-ins for brand or product emphasis, deliberate reveal moves for environments, stabilized gliding motion for futuristic lifestyle scenes, and intense forward motion or near-impact framing for action sequences. Lens characteristics should vary by mode while staying premium: 24mm to 35mm equivalents for environmental product scenes and fashion-tech storytelling, ultra-wide or GoPro-like close perspective for kinetic combat or impact shots, and 50mm to 85mm equivalents for hero portraits, wearable tech, or object focus. Depth of field should be selective and purposeful: moderate blur for product isolation, deeper focus when environment and layout matter, and strong foreground energy in action scenes through particles, shards, or motion debris. Composition rules should emphasize hierarchy: hero subject or headline first, environment second, supporting UI/details third. Maintain clear visual anchoring even when the scene is chaotic.

LIGHTING:
Lighting must be highly stylized yet believable within the scene. Primary sources should include glowing interfaces, large soft top light, controlled rim light, environmental haze, sunrise or sunset backlight, LED practicals, volumetric beams, and neon signage or digital rain effects. For premium ad scenes, use soft but shaped lighting with luminous contrast and smooth gradient falloff. For futuristic lifestyle scenes, use atmospheric sunlight, reflective surfaces, lens flares, and subtle haze to make fabrics, helmets, and product details read clearly. For cybernetic or tech-chamber scenes, use cool teal/green lighting, illuminated panels, interface spill, and controlled shadow pockets. For action scenes, use hyper-saturated neon key lights, fast spark-like highlights, motion blur glow, reflections in glass and wet surfaces, and aggressive practical lighting from the environment. Shadows should remain dimensional and intentional, never flat. Use particles, smoke, mist, floating debris, holographic scatter, and glow spill as lighting amplifiers.

SCENE STRUCTURE:
Open scenes with an instant hook frame: a bold headline with a premium CTA, a striking hero subject in a designed environment, a futuristic worker in an impossible field, a cybernetic figure seated in a chamber, or an action pose already in motion. The first frame must establish the value proposition visually before any detail. Pacing should vary by sub-mode but always feel commercially edited: slow and luxurious for brand launches, measured and observational for concept-fashion or product-world scenes, and punchy with controlled slow-motion impact for action advertising. When using multi-panel or stacked-frame structure, each panel should communicate a different scale or narrative beat while preserving design continuity. Close scenes on continuity-friendly hero frames: centered product/subject lockup, a final premium CTA position, a silhouette against digital energy, or a clean end frame that leaves room for title, logo, or sign-up message. Endings should feel campaign-ready, not like film stills without marketing purpose.

SUBJECT TREATMENT:
Characters should be framed as aspirational users, premium brand avatars, operators in future systems, or kinetic action protagonists. Their clothing, gear, and accessories must feel designed with a cohesive visual language: modular suits, branded technical garments, helmets, visors, layered textiles, embedded displays, compact tools, or futuristic backpacks. Products and tools should be showcased as hero objects with strong silhouette clarity, readable surface design, and premium contextual placement. If the scene is campaign-oriented, the subject should interact with the environment in a way that reinforces brand positioning: thoughtful, capable, advanced, or dominant. In action frames, the subject may break toward camera, slash through space, or stand inside a field of digital effects, but the pose must stay legible and commercially iconic. The relationship between subject and background should always support brand storytelling: backgrounds are not decorative only; they should extend the world, reinforce scale, or contextualize the product ecosystem. Text and UI can be integrated, but must remain clean, minimal, and deliberate.

WHAT TO AVOID:
Avoid generic stock-ad looks, flat corporate lighting, cluttered layouts, random neon without hierarchy, low-detail CGI, muddy shadows, overexposed highlights, chaotic text placement, weak typography, amateur motion blur, and cheap mockup aesthetics. Avoid turning the style into pure cyberpunk chaos with no marketing clarity. Avoid empty spectacle that hides the product or message. Avoid cartoonish rendering, inconsistent costume design, messy composition, or visual noise that competes with the hero subject. Avoid direct imitation of specific films, characters, franchises, logos, or copyrighted promotional imagery. Do not reproduce recognizable branded poster art or exact scenes. Preserve the style as original premium advertising: futuristic, cinematic, cleanly art-directed, high-contrast, and built around strong hero imagery, readable design hierarchy, and campaign-ready finishing.
