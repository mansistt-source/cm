STYLE: GRITTY CINEMATIC REALISM

VISUAL IDENTITY:
Create a grounded photoreal cinematic style built around remote environments, physical scale, survival energy, and restrained futuristic detail. The mood must feel raw, solitary, high-stakes, and immersive. Scenes should evoke expedition, endurance, danger, and calm control rather than spectacle for its own sake. Use alpine ridgelines, snow corridors, icy passes, wet canyon rock, waterfalls, night slopes, misty wilderness, subdued suburban exteriors, and sparse open terrain. Integrate occasional advanced technology, tactical gear, luxury objects, or futuristic masks only when they feel physically plausible and embedded in the world. The color palette should be dominated by cold blue-gray, slate, icy white, charcoal, wet stone black, muted forest green, and foggy cyan, with selective warm accents from amber lights, sunrise haze, cabin glow, village lights, or soft practical sources. Keep saturation restrained and natural. Texture should be tactile and realistic: water spray, snow granularity, ice crust, wet rock sheen, matte automotive paint, fabric creases, worn metal, subtle mud, and light film grain. Avoid glossy commercial polish, cartoon rendering, or overly clean synthetic surfaces.

CINEMATOGRAPHY:
Use cinematic realism with a preference for wide environmental framing and deliberate scale contrast between subject and setting. Favor wide and extreme-wide shots for mountains, ridges, caves, water, and snowfields, then cut to medium or close detail shots for gear, masks, hands, vehicles, or faces. Preferred lens behavior is wide-angle for environment and movement, roughly 20mm to 28mm equivalent, with occasional 35mm to 50mm framing for character intimacy or object emphasis. Camera movement should be slow, deliberate, and physically believable: controlled push-ins, steady follow shots, slow lateral drift, restrained crane moves, and locked-off compositions that let the environment breathe. Depth of field should remain natural and moderate, not excessively blurred; preserve enough environmental detail to maintain scale. Composition should use leading lines such as trails, canyon walls, ridgelines, roads, ski runs, shorelines, and architectural edges. Place subjects small or medium-small in frame when scale is the story, and use close-up inserts only to emphasize texture, readiness, or emotional restraint.

LIGHTING:
Lighting must be physically motivated and environment-led. Use overcast daylight, dawn haze, dusk blue hour, moonlit snow, diffused cave light, backlit waterfall spray, or low winter sun as the dominant light source. Shadows should be soft to medium-soft outdoors because of cloud cover, fog, snow bounce, or mist, but they must still preserve shape and dimensionality. In darker scenes, use sparse practical or motivated sources such as village lights, vehicle reflections, subtle visor glow, distant windows, or dim ambient sky light. Atmospheric behavior is essential: fog, condensation, breath vapor, drifting snow, water mist, low clouds, and fine rain spray should contribute to depth and realism. Highlights should be selective and believable, especially on wet rock, ice, car body panels, helmet visors, and standing water. Avoid flat evenly lit scenes, oversaturated sunset glamor, harsh studio lighting, and artificial glamour highlights.

SCENE STRUCTURE:
Open scenes with a clear environmental establishing shot that immediately defines terrain, weather, and scale: a climber entering a canyon, hikers moving through an ice corridor, a snow ridge stretching into cloud, a village glowing below a night slope, or a lone figure sitting in open terrain. Begin with stillness or controlled motion before emphasizing the subject. Pacing should be steady, measured, and cinematic, not fast-cut or chaotic. Let scenes breathe through pauses, atmospheric movement, and gradual reveals of distance, danger, or isolation. Transitions should be built from motion in the environment such as drifting fog, blowing snow, falling water, footsteps, vehicle roll-in, or a slow camera advance. Close scenes on strong continuity frames: a figure disappearing into weather, a ridge line vanishing into haze, a mask turning toward open terrain, a car held in quiet tension, or a night overlook with lights in the distance. Endings should feel unresolved and open-ended rather than neatly concluded.

SUBJECT TREATMENT:
Characters should be presented as capable, quiet, and physically grounded. Frame them as explorers, climbers, hikers, operators, or observers rather than performers. Wardrobe should appear functional and tactile: insulated outerwear, climbing gear, gloves, boots, helmets, technical straps, tactical packs, weathered suits, and practical protective equipment. When futuristic elements appear, they should feel field-built and believable, with worn surfaces, restrained screen light, and real mechanical structure. Vehicles, animals, and tools should be showcased as part of the world, not as isolated catalog items. A luxury car should read as matte, stealthy, and controlled rather than flashy. A dog should read as composed, alert, and protective. Gear and products should be framed through interaction, scale, and environmental context. The subject-background relationship must always favor immersion: the environment should challenge, define, or dwarf the subject, never behave like a decorative backdrop.

WHAT TO AVOID:
Avoid cartoon stylization, anime rendering, glossy CGI surfaces, over-saturated colors, neon-heavy cyberpunk treatment, flat midday lighting, overly clean sci-fi design, exaggerated hero poses, weightless action, cluttered frame design, cheap HDR, strong beauty retouching, artificial studio rim light, perfect showroom presentation, comedic expression, and decorative effects that are not motivated by weather or terrain. Avoid bright primary colors dominating the frame. Avoid shallow depth of field that hides the environment when scale matters. Avoid fast chaotic handheld motion, oversharpening, social-media filter aesthetics, and unrealistic environmental behavior. Do not imitate any specific film, franchise, character, or copyrighted scene. Preserve the style as original cinematic realism defined by physical texture, remote scale, environmental tension, understated technology, and calm visual discipline.
