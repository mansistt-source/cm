STYLE: COLD MONUMENTAL SCI-FI REALISM

VISUAL IDENTITY:
Create a cinematic sci-fi realism style with a cold, isolated, monumental atmosphere. The world must feel vast, hostile, silent, and physically overwhelming. Use fog-covered cities, flooded structures, ocean horizons, colossal waves, orbital space views, black voids, industrial aircraft interiors, remote frozen landscapes, abandoned houses, massive alien-scale architecture, and distant human figures. The emotional tone is restrained, ominous, lonely, and awe-driven, not heroic or colorful. The color palette must rely on deep teal, blue-gray, desaturated cyan, charcoal black, muted steel, cold white, pale amber practical light, and occasional warm orange sunlight. Maintain low saturation, heavy contrast, deep blacks, and selective highlights. Texture should be photoreal cinematic with visible atmosphere, moisture, fog, water reflections, smoke, fine film grain, realistic surfaces, worn metal, wet asphalt, ocean texture, and imperfect lens response. Avoid glossy clean sci-fi, colorful fantasy, cartoon rendering, or over-polished CGI.

CINEMATOGRAPHY:
Use large-scale cinematic compositions where the environment dominates the human subject. Favor wide shots, extreme wide shots, rear-facing figures, centered vanishing points, overhead structures, deep road perspectives, horizon-heavy frames, and subjects placed small against massive natural or cosmic forces. Camera movement should be slow, heavy, and deliberate: controlled push-ins, slow tracking shots, aerial drifts, locked-off symmetrical frames, and gradual reveals of scale. Use wide-angle lenses for environment scale, around 18mm–28mm equivalent, with occasional 35mm–50mm shots for grounded realism inside vehicles or interiors. Depth of field should be realistic and cinematic: foreground detail sharp when needed, background softened by haze, fog, distance, smoke, or water spray rather than artificial blur. Composition should use negative space aggressively: black voids, empty sky, empty ocean, foggy streets, and massive structures should occupy most of the frame. Keep human figures small, often from behind, facing the unknown.

LIGHTING:
Lighting must be low-key, atmospheric, and physically motivated. Primary light sources should come from fog-diffused daylight, overcast sky, practical neon signs, vehicle headlights, aircraft cabin lights, distant city glow, sunset through haze, moonlight, black-hole accretion glow, or reflected water light. Shadows should be deep, soft-edged, and heavy, with details partially swallowed by darkness or fog. Use practical lights sparingly: headlights, cabin strips, phone booth glow, signage, warning lights, distant windows, and small spacecraft lights. Atmospheric elements are essential: dense fog, mist, ocean spray, smoke, cloud layers, condensation, rain-wet surfaces, volumetric haze, and subtle lens bloom. Highlights should feel earned and localized, not evenly distributed. Avoid bright studio lighting, clean daylight exposure, colorful neon overload, flat shadows, and artificial rim lighting that does not match the environment.

SCENE STRUCTURE:
Open scenes with a wide establishing shot that immediately defines scale: a foggy road beneath elevated highways, a lone booth in open water, a human facing a wall of ocean, a spacecraft near a cosmic object, an aircraft cargo bay, or a remote figure walking through an impossible landscape. Begin with environmental stillness or slow motion before revealing the human subject. Pacing should be slow, tense, and cinematic, with long holds, minimal cutting, and gradual scale reveals. Use transitions through fog, darkness, water movement, cloud layers, vehicle interiors, or a slow camera drift into negative space. Close scenes on a frame that preserves mystery: a figure facing away, a glowing object isolated in darkness, a massive wave blocking the horizon, an empty foggy street, a spacecraft swallowed by void, or a structure disappearing into clouds. End frames should feel unresolved and continuous, not action-complete.

SUBJECT TREATMENT:
Characters should appear isolated, small, and physically grounded. Frame them from behind, waist-deep in water, walking alone, standing at the edge of scale, or partially obscured by fog, darkness, or environmental mass. Clothing and suits should be realistic, functional, and textured: astronaut suits, tactical outerwear, military fabrics, gloves, helmets, boots, utility gear, and weather-worn surfaces. Products, tools, vehicles, or machines should be shown as grounded physical objects with realistic wear, reflections, weight, and environmental interaction. Showcase objects through motivated light, close foreground placement, shallow realism, and surrounding scale rather than clean product presentation. Vehicles, tanks, spacecraft, booths, watches, and structures must feel heavy, used, and embedded in the scene. The background should not decorate the subject; it should threaten, dwarf, or isolate it.

WHAT TO AVOID:
Avoid colorful fantasy palettes, bright anime skies, cheerful lighting, superhero posing, glossy futuristic cleanliness, overdesigned neon cyberpunk, plastic CGI surfaces, excessive lens flares, busy action cutting, handheld chaos, comic-book framing, saturated reds and purples, clean corporate sci-fi, flat daylight, perfect symmetrical product-ad lighting, empty white backgrounds, decorative explosions, unrealistic water physics, soft dreamy romance, and cartoon-style exaggeration. Do not imitate any specific film, franchise, character, spacecraft design, or copyrighted scene. Preserve the style as original cinematic sci-fi realism built on cold atmosphere, monumental scale, human isolation, physical texture, restrained color, and slow visual tension.
