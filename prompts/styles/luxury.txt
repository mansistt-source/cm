STYLE: ULTRA-LUXURY CINEMATIC LIFESTYLE

VISUAL IDENTITY:
Create a premium luxury lifestyle style built around wealth, exclusivity, confidence, and aspirational status. The world should feel private, expensive, polished, and globally elite. Use superyachts, marina skylines, penthouse views, tailored suits, exotic cars, private pools, luxury interiors, mountain escapes, coastal prestige, and curated high-end environments. The emotional tone must project control, ease, power, and privilege rather than noise or chaos. The color palette should center on deep black, charcoal, espresso brown, cream, ivory, muted gold, metallic silver, soft champagne, rich tan leather, cool glass blue, and dusk sky tones. Use restrained saturation with selective highlights from city lights, sunset reflections, car paint, polished chrome, and water surfaces. Texture should feel refined and tactile: leather grain, tailored fabric, brushed metal, glossy paint, polished wood decking, marble, glass, still water, and subtle editorial film grain. Avoid cheap gloss, loud color overload, or cluttered visual design.

CINEMATOGRAPHY:
Use high-end editorial cinematography with a mix of architectural wides, composed lifestyle portraits, and hero vehicle angles. Favor confident compositions: low-angle shots that elevate buildings or cars, rear-view or side-profile shots that suggest dominance, centered or balanced compositions for luxury symmetry, and foreground framing through yacht rails, window panels, furniture, or vehicle body lines. Camera movement should feel smooth, controlled, and expensive: slow push-ins, elegant tracking shots, stabilized walking shots, slow reveals from behind shoulders, and deliberate orbit moves around cars or pools. Preferred lens behavior is 24mm to 35mm equivalent for environment-driven lifestyle scenes, 50mm to 85mm equivalent for portraits and fashion-led moments, and slightly wider hero lenses for vehicle showcase. Depth of field should be clean and selective: enough subject separation to feel premium, but never so blurred that the expensive environment disappears. Composition should clearly establish status markers such as skyline, yacht deck, luxury automobile silhouette, ocean horizon, or panoramic interior view.

LIGHTING:
Lighting must feel natural, premium, and art-directed. Primary light sources should include golden-hour sunlight, dusk skyline glow, window light from penthouses, marina reflections, cool city night illumination, subtle interior practicals, and clean luxury hospitality lighting. Shadows should be soft to medium-soft and flattering, with controlled contrast that preserves detail in dark suits, leather, car paint, and architectural materials. Use reflections deliberately on water, glass, chrome, and polished surfaces. Atmospheric effects may include light mist, snow haze, ocean air, dusk glow, subtle lens flare, or soft ambient bloom, but always with restraint. Night scenes should lean into sophisticated city sparkle, warm interior pools of light, and dark refined contrast, not harsh nightclub lighting. Avoid flat daylight snapshots, ugly overhead lighting, and messy mixed color temperatures.

SCENE STRUCTURE:
Open scenes with an immediate status-defining image: a yacht deck facing a skyline, a man overlooking a city from a penthouse, a supercar positioned in a dramatic environment, or a luxury pool with an iconic skyline beyond. Start with a strong establishing frame that communicates wealth before moving into personal detail. Pacing should feel slow, confident, and cinematic, not rushed. Let scenes breathe through elegant transitions, environmental reflections, and composed pauses. Move from environment to individual, or from object to owner, in a way that builds aspiration. Close scenes on iconic continuity frames: a hero car parked against an alpine sunset, a silhouette facing the skyline, a still luxury pool, a tailored figure beside a vehicle, or an intimate high-end interior moment. End frames should feel campaign-ready and memorable, leaving space for branding, taglines, or title overlays.

SUBJECT TREATMENT:
Characters should appear composed, confident, and effortlessly high-status. Frame them as owners, decision-makers, tastemakers, or elite travelers rather than performers. Wardrobe should read clearly as premium: tailored suits, leather jackets, refined knitwear, clean footwear, sunglasses, luxury luggage, and carefully chosen accessories. Pose direction should be relaxed but intentional: standing still with authority, seated with ease, looking over a city, leaning against a vehicle, stepping through an exclusive space, or engaging with a luxury environment in a restrained way. Vehicles, yachts, pools, luggage, penthouses, and other high-end objects should be showcased as hero assets with clean silhouette clarity, controlled reflections, and contextual prestige. Backgrounds should reinforce exclusivity: international cityscapes, marinas, snow-covered mountain ranges, upscale interiors, or coastal property views. Even unusual elements, such as exotic animals, should be treated as controlled symbols of rarity and opulence rather than chaos.

WHAT TO AVOID:
Avoid noisy street realism, casual everyday clutter, cheap interiors, oversaturated colors, flashy social-media edits, comedic posing, heavy action energy, chaotic crowds, low-detail CGI, flat corporate photography, bargain-luxury styling, bad reflections, overdone HDR, and harsh direct flash. Avoid random neon cyberpunk looks, gritty grime, heavy handheld shakiness, and clutter that weakens the sense of exclusivity. Avoid making the scene feel like a generic car listing or travel snapshot. Do not imitate any specific copyrighted ad campaign, poster, celebrity identity, or brand layout exactly. Preserve the style as original luxury visual language: calm, high-status, globally elite, compositionally refined, and built around aspirational environments, premium materials, and iconic hero framing.
