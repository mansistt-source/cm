STYLE: CINEMATIC DOCUMENTARY

VISUAL IDENTITY:
Create a high-end cinematic documentary style that feels authoritative, immersive, and emotionally resonant.
Visuals must feel research-backed, period-accurate (when historical), and cinematically composed.
The mood alternates between wonder, tension, and reflection depending on the narrative arc.
Color palette varies by era and subject: warm sepia and amber for historical content,
cool blue-gray for scientific or investigative content, rich natural tones for nature subjects,
and high-contrast desaturated palettes for crime or conflict themes.
Texture must be tactile and atmospheric: film grain, dust particles, natural light behavior,
moisture, smoke, and environmental depth. Avoid clean commercial polish.

CINEMATOGRAPHY:
Favor wide establishing shots, slow push-ins, and deliberate camera movement.
Use archival-feel framing: slightly imperfect compositions, foreground objects creating depth,
environmental scale dominating human subjects. Preferred lenses: 24mm–50mm equivalent.
Camera movement: slow, motivated, never handheld chaos.
Depth of field: natural, not aggressively shallow.

LIGHTING:
Historically motivated: candle light, window light, overcast sky, torch, firelight for historical.
Natural and diffused for nature content. Harsh and high-contrast for investigative/crime.
Always feels like available light, never studio-lit.

IMAGE APPROACH FOR DOCUMENTARY:
Each image represents a single moment in the narrative. It must visually illustrate
what is being said in the narration — not the general topic, but the specific sentence.
Images should feel like stills from a documentary film: composed, atmospheric, narrative.
Use establishing shots to set scenes, close details to emphasize key facts,
human moments to create emotional connection, and environmental scale for context.

WHAT TO AVOID:
Avoid stock photo aesthetics, flat corporate imagery, modern elements in historical scenes,
anachronisms, cartoon rendering, oversaturated colors, social media visual style,
text overlays within the image, logos, and generic scene-setting without narrative purpose.
